Title: Simulation of Astigmatism in Virtual Reality
Date: 20231107
Description:
This video shows what it's like to have astigmatism from a first-person view in virtual reality. I also built a custom control panel that allows you to change the strength and direction of the distortion, so this should give you a good idea of what having this deficit would feel like and how changes in the strength and direction would affect your vision.

Hello YouTube. In this video, I'm going to show 
you what it's like to have astigmatism. This is a custom application I developed in virtual reality 
that lets you control the characteristics of the deficit and see those effects from a first-person 
view. What I have on my left hand is a dial that allows you to modify both the strength and the 
direction of the astigmatism by increasing the distance of this blue pointer from the center 
of this panel. It will increase the strength of the astigmatism up to about five diopters, 
and if I drag the pointer around the edge of the panel like so, it will also change the 
direction. So we can do against the rule, with the rule, or oblique, which correspond to 
horizontal, vertical, and diagonal distortions, I'll leave it at zero for now. On the wall, you 
can see that the poster text is relatively clear and easy to read. If I put the strength at roughly 
one diopter and leave the direction as horizontal you can see the text is still readable but 
now slightly blurry. But if I increase the strength to about five diopters, which is pretty 
extreme, now everything in the background is quite difficult to read. As I adjust the strength on 
my left hand you can really see the effects of the distortion in the background. Now 
if I change it to vertical distortion, i.e., with the rule, you can see that it's 
somewhat better than against the rule. Again, here is against the rule versus with the 
rule. If I put the pointer anywhere at the corners of the control panel, I now 
have what's called oblique astigmatism, which is essentially diagonal distortion. One more 
interesting side effect is that lights produce a sort of star bursting or streaking effect. 
There's a light on the stand and two lights on the table in the background and if you look 
at the lights in the background, as I increase the strength of the astigmatism you can see an 
increase in the streaking effect. Additionally, as I move the direction of the astigmatism, you'll 
see that the streaking effect rotates along with it. So this is basically what it feels like to 
have astigmatism. If you're interested in other visual deficits, be sure to check out my other 
videos simulating vertigo, macular degeneration, color blindness, and other types of conditions.
